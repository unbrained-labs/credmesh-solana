# Inspiration — Architectural Blueprint

References used directly for this concept. Specifically what was lifted from each.

## Primary references

1. **Le Corbusier — Villa Savoye section drawings (1929)**
   https://dome.mit.edu/handle/1721.3/40450 — Fondation Le Corbusier holdings
   *Took:* the cyan blueprint paper / cream ink pairing; the way numeric callouts sit ABOVE a hairline rule; the convention of multiple titles per drawing (alternative title + sheet number). Drove the dashboard's color system directly.

2. **Carlo Scarpa — Negozio Olivetti, Venice (1957–58)**
   https://www.ponteonline.com/en/lot-details/auction/514/lot/80
   *Took:* axonometric construction-detail sketches of stone joints alongside the main plan; the legibility of dimension annotations even when annotated by hand. This is why the key diagram is an exploded axon of three plates with leader-line callouts.

3. **Carlo Scarpa — Castelvecchio Museum drawing-table sheet**
   http://catalogo.beniculturali.it/detail/HistoricOrArtisticProperty/0500321931A
   *Took:* the layered, palimpsest feel of an architect's working sheet — handwritten annotations laid over printed plates. Drove the use of Architects Daughter for the margin notes ("verify oracle pubkey before close ✓ done 2026-04-22").

4. **Foster + Partners — GLA Headquarters, exploded axonometric (1999)**
   https://collections.vam.ac.uk/item/O45964/exploded-axonometric-of-building-elements-design-foster-and-partners/
   *Took:* the exploded axonometric as a vocabulary for explaining a system's parts. The key diagram on the right column is built directly on this — three plates separated along a single bolt axis with leader-line labels.

5. **Charles & Ray Eames — Case Study House #8 elevation/details (1949)**
   https://www.loc.gov/item/97519377 (LOC blueprint scan)
   https://eamesfoundation.org/works/case-study-house-8
   *Took:* the original cyanotype + colored pencil aesthetic; the use of off-the-shelf parts called out by their catalog name. Drove the convention of naming each program (P‑01 VAULT / P‑02 BEAM / P‑03 WORKER) as if it were a part number on a construction drawing.

## Pattern / convention references

6. **Exploded axonometric drawing primer (ZORA)**
   https://zora-group.com/exploded-axonometric-diagram/
   *Took:* the rule that separated components must maintain their spatial relationship along a common axis; the use of dashed leader lines for the assembly axis. The bolt-axis dashed line in both the logo and the key diagram comes from this.

7. **CityTech ARCH 1231 — Structural Studies and Exploded Axon**
   https://openlab.citytech.cuny.edu/montgomeryarch1231fa2018/2018/11/13/structural-studies-and-exploded-axon/
   *Took:* the convention of pairing scaled sections with annotations showing rules of thumb and calculations. Drove the secondary metrics row (share price / outstanding / fee curve / agents) being annotated with sub-detail rather than just a number.

## Conceptual references (no direct visual lift)

8. **Rem Koolhaas — *S, M, L, XL* (1995)**
   *Took:* the tone — that a system's diagrams can be the system's identity; that dense data presented like a document is more honest than dressed-up dataviz.

9. **Bjarke Ingels — *Yes is More* (2009)**
   *Took:* the comic-strip-as-section legibility — every component can be named and numbered without losing the picture. Drove the section markers (Ⓐ Ⓑ Ⓒ Ⓓ Ⓔ) as a unifying language for the four data zones + key diagram.

10. **Le Corbusier's Modulor diagrams**
    *Took:* the willingness to print ratios as the primary visual element, not as an annotation. Drove "VESSEL CAPACITY ▸ MEASURED 2026-05-03 14:08:22 UTC" sitting under the TVL number as a peer to the value, not a subordinate footnote.
