# Concept 02 — industrial-throughput

Credit as fluid mechanics. The brand-world is a 1960s Texas Instruments process-control cabinet meets a nuclear reactor's piping diagram. Everything is a P&ID schematic — pipes, valves, manifolds, pressure gauges, flange callouts. Typography is IBM Plex Mono exclusively (or Berkeley Mono). Color palette is brass, oxidized iron, fume yellow, warning red — NO blue, NO purple, NO glow.

Charts are flow rates and pressure curves, never line graphs. The logo is a schematic glyph — a manifold cross-section, a flange, a relief valve. Numeric displays use a mechanical-counter aesthetic (the kind on a 1970s gas pump). The dashboard reads like a control panel.

References: Soviet industrial signage, Honeywell process control catalogs 1965-1980, NASA Apollo flight plans, Vignelli for Knoll, Tufte's process-control charts, the cover of "How to Make a Spaceship."

Credit, in this world, is throughput — measured in barrels-per-second, gated by relief valves, monitored by gauges that have weight.
